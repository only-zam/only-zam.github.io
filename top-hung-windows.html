Types of windows by operation

  1. Top Hung or Awning windows

    Top hung windows are hinged at the top and open outward from the bottom, making them ideal for areas where space is limited. They are perfect for bathrooms, kitchens, or high-rise buildings, as their design prevents rainwater from entering while allowing fresh air to flow in.

  2. Double Hung

    They feature two movable sashes that slide vertically within the frame, allowing for flexible ventilation options. They open from the top or bottom, are easy to clean and provide great ventilation options. Double-hung windows however require regular maintenance

  3. Sliding

    Sliding windows are a popular choice for modern homes and offices in Kenya due to their space-saving design and ease of operation

  4. Casement or Side Hung

    Casement windows either swing left or right using a crank, similar to a door. They are perfect for capturing breezes and maximizing airflow, making them ideal for living rooms, bedrooms, and other frequently used spaces.

  5. Fixed Windows

    Although permanently fixed windows do not provide airflow, they provide great weather resistance and create unobstructed views of your landscape. Fixed windows are ideal for areas where ventilation is not a priority, such as hallways, staircases, or decorative features. They typically have no moving parts and are thus less expensive compared to other window options

Types of windows by location

  • Corner windows: Maximizing Light and Views

    Corner windows are a striking architectural feature where two window units meet at a 90-degree angle, creating a seamless glass corner that floods interiors with natural light and offers panoramic views. Aluminium window frames are a popular choice for corner windows due to their strength and slim profiles, which maximize glass area.

  • Bay windows

    Bay windows are typically composed of three panels â€” a large central window flanked by two smaller side windows that project outward from a home's facade. They're popular in living rooms and dining areas as they're known to flood rooms with plenty of natural light while offering expansive views. They can be triangular, rectangular, or polygonal in shape.

  • Gable windows

    These type of windows are named gable because they're typically located at the gable end of a sloped roof. They enhance a home's architectural character by complementing your roofline's outlook while providing excellent ventilation and functional lighting. Gable windows are typically found in traditional designs, cottages, or modern homes

  • Dormer windows: Style and Function for Sloped Roofs

    Dormer windows are vertical windows that project from a sloping roof, adding headroom, natural light, and ventilation to attic or loft spaces. These architectural features combine practicality with visual appeal, breaking up large roof planes while creating charming exterior details. They're particularly valuable for transforming dark attic spaces into functional living areas while enhancing curb appeal.

  • Skylights: Natural Illumination from Above

    Skylights are roof-mounted windows that flood interior spaces with passive overhead sunlight, creating brighter, more inviting rooms while reducing reliance on artificial lighting. These innovative glazing solutions work particularly well in windowless areas like bathrooms, hallways, and attic conversions. Modern skylights come in fixed, vented, or tubular designs, with aluminium frames enabling sleek, functional and low-profile installations.

  • Clerestory windows: Elevated Light & Privacy

    Clerestory windows are high-placed horizontal windows that illuminate interior spaces while maintaining privacy and wall space below. Positioned above eye level, these architectural features bathe rooms in natural light without compromising security. They're ideal for modern and mid-century architecture.
